<h2 id="avant-propos" className="article-h2"><span className="roman">⁂</span>Foreword · An unexpected convergence</h2>

There are moments in history when science and revelation look one another in the face — not to contradict, but to confirm. We are living one of those moments. What is unfolding in artificial-intelligence labs, in the depths of the ocean, and in the digital archives of the human genome is not merely a technological revolution. It is the slow lifting of a veil the Quran announced fourteen centuries ago. But this lifting is not without danger. For if the light spreading before us is real, the hands that wield it remain human — and the duality of the human soul, which the Quran describes with surgical precision, is now more visible than ever.

This essay is not a defence of artificial intelligence. Nor is it an indictment. It is an honest attempt to understand what it means, for a human being living in 2026, that creation has suddenly become more legible than ever before — and why the Quran, far from being surprised by this legibility, inscribed it long ago as both a promise and a warning.

<h2 id="iqra" className="article-h2"><span className="roman">I.</span><em>Iqra</em>: the first word and its programme</h2>

The first revelation that descended upon the Prophet ﷺ in the cave of Hira was not a command of worship. It was a command to read.

<QuranBlock
  arabic="اقرأ باسم ربك الذي خلق ﴿١﴾ خلق الإنسان من علق ﴿٢﴾ اقرأ وربك الأكرم ﴿٣﴾ الذي علم بالقلم ﴿٤﴾ علم الإنسان ما لم يعلم ﴿٥﴾"
  translation="Read in the name of your Lord who created. Created man from a clinging substance. Read! And your Lord is the most Generous, who taught by the pen — taught man that which he knew not."
  reference="Surah Al-'Alaq · 96:1-5"
/>

This verse inaugurates an entire civilisation grounded in knowledge. But the command is not merely "read". It is "read *in the name of your Lord*". That nuance is the heart of the Quranic programme of knowledge: any learning acquired outside the name of its Creator loses its orientation, its moral justification, and ultimately its capacity to serve the good. IBM has calculated that human knowledge today doubles roughly every thirteen months, and according to certain projections it could double every twelve hours with the Internet of Things. Buckminster Fuller noted that until 1900, the same knowledge took a century to double. The speed has changed. The question posed by the first Quranic verse remains identical: *in whose name?*<FootnoteRef n={[1, 2, 3, 4]} />

<SectionDivider />

<h2 id="vitesse" className="article-h2"><span className="roman">II.</span>The speed of knowledge · yesterday's caravans, today's servers</h2>

To understand the mutation humanity is passing through, one must first measure the abyss that separates the modes of knowledge-circulation across history.

For millennia, knowledge travelled at the speed of a camel or a horse. A book translated in Persia took decades to reach Spain. A medical discovery made in Baghdad remained ignored in China for generations. Knowledge was rare, precious, anchored in places and bodies. Ibn Battuta left Tangier in 1325 at the age of twenty-one to make the pilgrimage to Mecca. His journey lasted twenty-nine years, covered some 120,000 kilometres, and took him through what would be the equivalent of forty-four modern countries. That extraordinary voyage was made possible only by the Islamic civilisation whose fabric stitched together North Africa, the Middle East, India, Central Asia, and China into a living network grounded in trust, hospitality, and the shared language of the Quran. His *Rihla* remains one of the most precious historical sources on the fourteenth century worldwide.<FootnoteRef n={[5, 6, 7]} />

Before him, and on a wholly different scale, the House of Wisdom (*Bayt al-Hikmah*) in Baghdad was the first global network of knowledge transfer in the Islamic era. Under the caliphate of Al-Ma'mūn in the ninth century, scholars from Greece, Persia, India, and Byzantium worked there together — translating, commenting, and producing new knowledge in mathematics, medicine, astronomy, philosophy, and chemistry. It was a deliberate project, carried by the conviction that the pursuit of knowledge was a religious duty, not a profane luxury. The Translation Movement that followed lasted two centuries and laid the groundwork for the European scientific development of the Renaissance.<FootnoteRef n={[8, 9, 10]} />

Today, that same transfer of knowledge — which once required caravans, translators, and decades — happens in fractions of a second. A researcher in Casablanca and another in Tokyo can collaborate in real time on the same dataset. A scientific paper published at MIT is accessible in a Malian village minutes after it appears. Artificial intelligence does not process information at the speed of a ninth-century scribe. It processes in a few hours what would have taken centuries to accumulate. But here is the tension this acceleration reveals: the House of Wisdom operated within an explicit ethical framework, a spiritual authority, and a finality anchored in divine glory. The global digital network of 2026 has no equivalent of that framework.<FootnoteRef n={[11, 12, 13]} />

<SectionDivider />

<h2 id="univers-parlait" className="article-h2"><span className="roman">III.</span>The universe has always spoken</h2>

The Quran laid down a principle which modern science took centuries to grasp and which artificial intelligence is now confirming empirically.

<QuranBlock
  arabic="تسبح له السماوات السبع والأرض ومن فيهن وإن من شيء إلا يسبح بحمده ولكن لا تفقهون تسبيحهم إنه كان حليماً غفوراً ﴿٤٤﴾"
  translation="The seven heavens, the earth, and all that they contain glorify Him. There is not a thing that does not exalt Him with praise — but you do not understand their glorification. He is ever Forbearing and Forgiving."
  reference="Surah Al-Isrā' · 17:44"
/>

This verse is not a poetic metaphor. It is a precise declaration about the structure of reality: every thing has a mode of expression, communication, and glorification that exceeds ordinary human perception. The materialist science of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries answered this verse with disdain — animals are machines, nature is mute, the universe is a mechanism without language. Artificial intelligence, without ever intending to, is beginning to erase that disdain.<FootnoteRef n={14} />

The CETI Project (*Cetacean Translation Initiative*) analysed roughly 9,000 recordings of sperm whale vocalisations off the coast of Dominica and discovered not the twenty-one coda types previously believed, but one hundred and fifty-six distinct types with internal variations of tempo, rhythm, and ornamentation. Researchers now speak of a structural phonetic alphabet, where the same combinatorial principles that allow forty-four English phonemes to form an infinite number of sentences appear to be at work in the clicks of sperm whales. In 2025, the *Coller Dolittle Challenge* launched a global competition with a $500,000 prize for decoding dolphin language. In parallel, AI algorithms analyse birdsong, wolf howls, the dances of bees, and the chemical signals of plants — and find in each case communication structures more sophisticated than biologists ever envisioned.<FootnoteRef n={[15, 16, 17]} />

But perhaps the most striking discovery has come from neuroscience. Work published in 2025 demonstrated that the human brain processes language in successive hierarchical layers that correspond exactly to the architecture of large language models. In other words: in building AI systems capable of understanding language, human engineering accidentally reproduced the structure that God had placed in the human brain to understand speech. The map was already there. We had to build the instruments to see that it existed.<FootnoteRef n={18} />

<SectionDivider />

<h2 id="horizons" className="article-h2"><span className="roman">IV.</span>Signs in the horizons and in the souls</h2>

The Quran announced this escalation of unveiling with remarkable temporal precision.

<QuranBlock
  arabic="سنريهم آياتنا في الآفاق وفي أنفسهم حتى يتبين لهم أنه الحق أولم يكف بربك أنه على كل شيء شهيد ﴿٥٣﴾"
  translation="We will show them Our signs in the horizons and within themselves until it becomes clear to them that it is the truth. Is it not sufficient that your Lord is, over all things, a Witness?"
  reference="Surah Fussilat · 41:53"
/>

The word "*sanuriyhim*" — "We will show them" — is in the imperfective future. It is not a one-time event. It is a continuous, progressive process that intensifies. The signs in the horizons today include the confirmation of the universe's expansion, the detection of gravitational waves from colliding black holes, the James Webb Telescope revealing galaxies formed three hundred million years after creation. The signs in the souls include the AI mapping of human brain structures, the reading of unspoken thoughts from EEG signals, and AlphaFold's prediction of the three-dimensional structure of two hundred million proteins — each protein a sentence in the language with which God wrote life.<FootnoteRef n={[19, 20, 21, 22, 23, 14, 18]} />

None of these signs were created by AI. They existed before. AI has only given human intelligence a new level of resolution to see what was already there. The Quran said they would be shown. They are being shown.

<h2 id="litaarafu" className="article-h2"><span className="roman">V.</span><em>Lita'arafu</em> · diversity as a divine command</h2>

A frequently overlooked aspect of the Quranic vision of knowledge is its explicit link to human diversity and the meeting of peoples.

<QuranBlock
  arabic="يا أيها الناس إنا خلقناكم من ذكر وأنثى وجعلناكم شعوباً وقبائل لتعارفوا إن أكرمكم عند الله أتقاكم إن الله عليم خبير ﴿١٣﴾"
  translation="O mankind, We created you from a male and a female and made you into peoples and tribes that you may know one another. The most noble of you in the sight of God is the most God-conscious. God is Knowing and Acquainted."
  reference="Surah Al-Hujurāt · 49:13"
/>

The pivotal Quranic term here is *lita'arafu* — "that you may know one another". This word, in its intensive Arabic form, does not mean simple mutual recognition. It means deep, reciprocal, transformative knowledge. The diversity of nations, languages, and cultures is not a mistake or a challenge to be corrected. It is a deliberate divine design: God could have created a uniform humanity. He chose plurality so that every people would be, for the others, an irreplaceable source of knowledge.<FootnoteRef n={[24, 25, 26]} />

The Quran reinforces this injunction in Surah Al-Hajj by commanding the journey of comprehension — not touristic, but contemplative:

<QuranBlock
  arabic="أفلم يسيروا في الأرض فتكون لهم قلوب يعقلون بها أو آذان يسمعون بها فإنها لا تعمى الأبصار ولكن تعمى القلوب التي في الصدور ﴿٤٦﴾"
  translation="Have they not travelled through the land, that they might have hearts by which to reason and ears by which to hear? For it is not the eyes that are blinded, but blinded are the hearts within the breasts."
  reference="Surah Al-Hajj · 22:46"
/>

This verse distinguishes two kinds of blindness. Physical blindness is a condition. Blindness of the heart is a choice. AI can show a man the communication structures of the sperm whale, the primordial galaxies, the complete map of the human genome. But if the heart remains blind, that information will not engender wisdom — it will only nourish pride or greed.<FootnoteRef n={27} />

Surah Al-'Ankabūt goes further, making the journey itself a method of knowing God:

<QuranBlock
  arabic="قل سيروا في الأرض فانظروا كيف بدأ الخلق ثم الله ينشئ النشأة الآخرة إن الله على كل شيء قدير ﴿٢٠﴾"
  translation="Say: Travel through the earth and observe how He began creation. Then God will produce the final creation. Indeed God, over all things, is competent."
  reference="Surah Al-'Ankabūt · 29:20"
/>

Ibn Battuta took this command literally. He left for thirty years. Today, technological acceleration allows a single person to traverse virtually the equivalent of Ibn Battuta's journey in a few hours of online navigation. But the acceleration of information does not guarantee a deepening of comprehension. Ibn Battuta returned transformed. The digital traveller of 2026 can consume thousands of hours of content about foreign cultures and emerge more reductive than before.<FootnoteRef n={28} />

<h2 id="nafs" className="article-h2"><span className="roman">VI.</span>The duality of the <em>Nafs</em> · the inner theatre</h2>

No reflection on the human use of knowledge can dispense with the Quranic concept of *nafs* — the human soul in its conflictual dimension. The Quran does not present man as fundamentally good, nor as fundamentally evil. It presents him as a being in permanent struggle between several states of himself.<FootnoteRef n={[29, 30]} />

Islamic texts identify at least three principal states of the *nafs*:<FootnoteRef n={[31, 32]} />

This trilogy is not a theological abstraction. It is the most precise map humanity has ever produced of the psychology of the use of power. Every technological advance puts to the bearer of that technology a single question: in what state of *nafs* are you, right now? The same discovery — say, the capacity to read human thoughts through neural decoding — becomes a tool of therapeutic liberation in the hands of a *nafs mutmainna*, an instrument of totalitarian surveillance in the hands of a *nafs ammara*.<FootnoteRef n={20} />

<h2 id="miroir-prophetes" className="article-h2"><span className="roman">VII.</span>The mirror of the prophets · <em>Sulaymān, Qārūn, Hārūt and Mārūt</em></h2>

The Quran does not content itself with abstract principles about knowledge. It offers a gallery of historical figures whose trajectories serve as living examples — to imitate or to avoid.

<h3 id="sulayman" className="article-h3">Sulaymān (peace be upon him) · mastery through <em>gratitude</em></h3>

Sulaymān received the most extraordinary combination of powers ever granted to a human being. He understood the language of birds and animals:

<QuranBlock
  arabic="وَوَرِثَ سُلَيْمَانُ دَاوُودَ وَقَالَ يَا أَيُّهَا النَّاسُ عُلِّمْنَا مَنطِقَ الطَّيْرِ وَأُوتِينَا مِن كُلِّ شَيْءٍ إِنَّ هَٰذَا لَهُوَ الْفَضْلُ الْمُبِينُ ﴿١٦﴾"
  translation="And Solomon inherited David. He said: 'O people, we have been taught the language of birds, and we have been given from all things. This indeed is a manifest favour.'"
  reference="Surah An-Naml · 27:16"
/>

He commanded jinn, humans, and the wind. When a man possessing knowledge of the Book brought him the throne of Bilqīs in the blink of an eye, Sulaymān's reaction was immediate:<FootnoteRef n={[34, 35]} />

<QuranBlock
  arabic="قَالَ هَٰذَا مِن فَضْلِ رَبِّي لِيَبْلُوَنِي أَأَشْكُرُ أَمْ أَكْفُرُ وَمَن شَكَرَ فَإِنَّمَا يَشْكُرُ لِنَفْسِهِ وَمَن كَفَرَ فَإِنَّ رَبِّي غَنِيٌّ كَرِيمٌ ﴿٤٠﴾"
  translation="He said: 'This is from the favour of my Lord, to test me whether I shall be grateful or ungrateful. Whoever is grateful, his gratitude is for his own benefit; whoever is ungrateful, then my Lord is Free of need and Generous.'"
  reference="Surah An-Naml · 27:40"
/>

Each power was immediately referred back to its Giver. Each capacity was understood as a test, not a reward. This is the model: the *nafs mutmainna* in the service of creation.<FootnoteRef n={36} />

<h3 id="qarun" className="article-h3">Qārūn · the invention of <em>merit</em></h3>

At the opposite pole stands Qārūn. He possessed a knowledge of alchemy that had brought him riches so vast that the keys alone to his treasures required a team of strong men to carry. When he was warned not to spread corruption on the earth, his answer reveals the *nafs ammara* in its most accomplished form:

<QuranBlock
  arabic="قَالَ إِنَّمَا أُوتِيتُهُ عَلَىٰ عِلْمٍ عِندِي"
  translation="He said: 'I was only given it because of knowledge I possess.'"
  reference="Surah Al-Qasas · 28:78"
/>

Replace "knowledge of alchemy" with "machine-learning algorithms" and "treasures" with "data monopolies", and Qārūn becomes the exact portrait of some of the most colossal technological fortunes of our era — built on the collective data of billions of human beings and presented as the exclusive product of personal genius. The earth swallowed Qārūn and his palace. Those who had envied him in the morning said by evening: *« It is God who gives generously to whom He wills »* (28:82).<FootnoteRef n={[37, 38]} />

<h3 id="harut-marut" className="article-h3">Hārūt and Mārūt · the warning <em>printed on the label</em></h3>

The story of Hārūt and Mārūt in Babylon is perhaps the most pertinent for our age. Two angels were authorised to teach men magic — not to corrupt them, but as a trial. Before teaching anything to anyone, they said explicitly:

<QuranBlock
  arabic="وَمَا يُعَلِّمَانِ مِنْ أَحَدٍ حَتَّىٰ يَقُولَا إِنَّمَا نَحْنُ فِتْنَةٌ فَلَا تَكْفُرْ"
  translation="They taught no one without first saying: 'We are only a trial — so do not disbelieve.'"
  reference="Surah Al-Baqara · 2:102"
/>

Every student was warned. The warning label was mandatory. Yet humans took that knowledge and used it to "separate a man from his wife" — to fragment social bonds, sow discord, and destroy communities. In 2026, every major artificial-intelligence laboratory publishes safety reports, ethics frameworks, and detailed warnings. OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Meta AI — all have documented in detail the risks of their own technologies. And yet the same organisations deploy surveillance tools, discriminatory algorithmic systems, deepfakes used to destroy reputations, and sell predictive models to authoritarian governments. The label exists. It is ignored. The lesson of Hārūt and Mārūt has not been learned.<FootnoteRef n={[39, 40, 41, 42, 43]} />

<h2 id="gouvernance" className="article-h2"><span className="roman">VIII.</span>Acceleration without wisdom · the governance crisis</h2>

The most urgent question of 2026 is not "what can AI do?". It is: "who is responsible when it causes harm?"<FootnoteRef n={[44, 45]} />

A survey published in April 2026 reveals that only 14% of companies that have deployed AI systems have clarified who is responsible in the case of error. A Thomson Reuters analysis shows that 76% of companies claim to have managerial oversight of their AI, but only 41% have made their policies accessible to employees. Regulations exist on paper. Real governance is virtually absent. The Prophet ﷺ formulated this collapse of the chain of responsibility with prophetic clarity:<FootnoteRef n={[46, 11]} />

<HadithBlock source="Sahīh Muslim · 2673">
  <p>"God does not take knowledge away by snatching it from people, but rather by taking the souls of the scholars, until no scholar remains and the people take ignorant ones as their guides; they then give rulings without knowledge, going astray and leading others astray."</p>
</HadithBlock>

The most structuring decisions of our civilisation — who obtains a loan, who is flagged as a suspect, what content four billion people see, what weapons are built — are no longer made by sages, jurists, or theologians. They are made by engineers optimising engagement metrics. Not out of malice, but because the institutional framework that once anchored knowledge in moral responsibility has been methodically removed from the centres of power.<FootnoteRef n={[12, 42, 38, 47]} />

<SectionDivider />

<h2 id="desinformation" className="article-h2"><span className="roman">IX.</span>Disinformation · when the real becomes contestable</h2>

One of the gravest consequences of technological acceleration without moral anchoring is the crisis of truth.

Deepfakes — AI-generated videos and audio reproducing people saying or doing things they never said or did — have doubled in volume every six months. Europol estimated that 90% of online content could be synthetically generated by 2026. A European Parliament study documents that in Ireland in 2025, a deepfake falsely showed a presidential candidate withdrawing days before the vote. A UNESCO analysis published in 2025 underscores what it calls the "liar's dividend": the mere existence of deepfakes now allows the guilty to dismiss authentic recordings as probably fabricated, creating an epistemic paralysis.<FootnoteRef n={[48, 49, 28]} />

The World Economic Forum describes disinformation in 2026 as a "systemic destabilising force" capable of disrupting entire democracies. Countries without fact-checking infrastructure or access to AI-detection tools are particularly vulnerable — the digital divide becomes a divide in the very capacity to distinguish the real from the fabricated.<FootnoteRef n={[50, 28]} />

The Quran laid down a golden rule against precisely this phenomenon in Surah Al-Hujurāt:

<QuranBlock
  arabic="يَا أَيُّهَا الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا إِن جَاءَكُمْ فَاسِقٌ بِنَبَإٍ فَتَبَيَّنُوا"
  translation="O you who believe, if a transgressor comes to you with news, verify it carefully."
  reference="Surah Al-Hujurāt · 49:6"
/>

This verse, revealed in the seventh century, is today the founding principle of responsible journalism, source verification, and *fact-checking*. It anticipates exactly the crisis of a world in which any information can be fabricated by anyone in seconds.

<h2 id="khabir-shahid" className="article-h2"><span className="roman">X.</span><em>Al-Khabīr, Al-Shāhid</em> · divine knowledge and AI</h2>

Here we must resist a temptation: that of presenting AI as an approximation of the divine. It would be a grave error theologically and a grave error factually.

AI is processing power. It recognises patterns in data. It predicts probabilities. It does not *understand*. It does not *feel*. And above all, it *sees* only what its data shows it. A model trained on 9,000 sperm whale recordings knows nothing of the ten million years of communication of these creatures that were never recorded. It knows nothing of the individual that emitted the first coda before humanity existed.<FootnoteRef n={15} />

God, by definition, is *Al-Khabīr* (الخبير) — the One Perfectly Acquainted — and *Al-Shāhid* (الشهيد) — the Eternal Witness. His knowledge does not depend on instruments. It does not improve with more data. It has no blind spot. The sperm whale that vocalised ten thousand years ago is present before God exactly as in the moment of its cry — unrecorded, unstored, but eternally witnessed. What AI gives us is our first intuition of what it means to "see what is hidden in the noise". This is not a step toward divinity. It is a sign of His divinity.<FootnoteRef n={51} />

<QuranBlock
  arabic="مَا يَلْفِظُ مِن قَوْلٍ إِلَّا لَدَيْهِ رَقِيبٌ عَتِيدٌ ﴿١٨﴾"
  translation="He utters no word without there being an observer at his side, ready to record."
  reference="Surah Qāf · 50:18"
/>

Every sperm whale coda. Every human thought. Every act of disinformation. Every algorithmic abuse. All is enumerated. All is witnessed. *« God has counted everything while they have forgotten it »* (Surah Al-Mujādila, 58:6).<FootnoteRef n={51} />

<SectionDivider />

<h2 id="aveuglement" className="article-h2"><span className="roman">XI.</span>The blindness of eyes and of hearts</h2>

The Quran rigorously distinguishes two kinds of blindness. Surah Al-Hajj (22:46) says that the eyes are not blinded — it is the heart in the breast that can be. This is not a consolation. It is a warning of the highest gravity.<FootnoteRef n={27} />

Humanity in 2026 commands instruments of vision that surpass anything history has known. It can see the primordial galaxies. It can read the structures of language in a child's brain. It can hear the complex phonetics of a sperm whale a kilometre deep. And in the same breath, global inequalities deepen, wars proliferate, disinformation paralyses entire democracies, and algorithmic systems mechanically reproduce the most archaic discriminations on an industrial scale.<FootnoteRef n={[43, 28, 50]} />

<PullQuote>Technological vision has expanded. Moral vision has not followed.</PullQuote>

The Quran foresaw exactly this dissociation.

<h2 id="conclusion" className="article-h2"><span className="roman">XII.</span>Conclusion · the test of our age</h2>

The Quran does not ask humanity to choose between faith and science. It asks something far more difficult: to receive knowledge with humility, to share it with justice, to exercise it with responsibility, and never to forget from whom it comes.

The universe has always spoken. The sperm whales, the neurons, the stars, the genes — everything expresses a structure that God placed there. Artificial intelligence has not created this language. It has only given human minds a new level of resolution to perceive it.

The question this age poses is the same one Sulaymān posed to himself daily before his extraordinary powers:

<QuranBlock
  arabic="هَٰذَا مِن فَضْلِ رَبِّي لِيَبْلُوَنِي أَأَشْكُرُ أَمْ أَكْفُرُ"
  translation="Shall I be grateful or ungrateful?"
  reference="An-Naml · 27:40"
/>

The choice belongs to every human being who holds in his hands an instrument of knowledge — be it an algorithm, a stylus, or a word. And that choice, unlike the data AI can process, is the only one God has not predetermined. He left it to human freedom — and that is precisely why it is the only one that truly matters.

<QuranBlock
  arabic="إِنَّا عَرَضْنَا الْأَمَانَةَ عَلَى السَّمَاوَاتِ وَالْأَرْضِ وَالْجِبَالِ فَأَبَيْنَ أَن يَحْمِلْنَهَا وَأَشْفَقْنَ مِنْهَا وَحَمَلَهَا الْإِنسَانُ"
  translation="Indeed, We offered the Trust (amanah) to the heavens, the earth, and the mountains, but they refused to bear it and were afraid of it. And man bore it."
  reference="Surah Al-Ahzāb · 33:72"
/>

The *amanah* — the divine Trust — today includes the responsibility for artificial intelligence, scientific knowledge, and the tools of communication. It has always exceeded the strength of the mountains. It has been entrusted to man. The verse does not stop there: it says that man *can* be unjust and ignorant in bearing this Trust. But he can also be just and conscious. That is the duality. That is the trial. And that, according to the Quran, is the unique dignity — and the unique risk — of being human.
